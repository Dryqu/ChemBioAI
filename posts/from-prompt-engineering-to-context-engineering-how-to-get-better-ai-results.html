From Prompt Engineering to Context Engineering: How to Get Better AI Results

December 24, 2025 • By Yi Qu

You can write a beautiful, detailed prompt and still get a disappointing answer. Not because you did it “wrong,” but because the real lever has shifted.

The new advantage isn’t longer prompts. It’s context engineering—and understanding where your context should live.

1) Why “Perfect Prompts” Matter Less Than They Used To

Early AI systems needed extremely detailed instructions to perform well. You had to spell out everything step by step.

Modern models have absorbed common interaction patterns from millions of users. Platforms now add defaults, guardrails, and tools around the model.

The problem: “acceptable” is not the same as reliable. If you want outputs that consistently fit your needs, you need a different approach.

2) The Real Shift: Context Engineering

Traditional prompting focuses on wording:

“How do I say this so the model understands?”

Context engineering reframes the question:

“What information does the model need so it doesn’t have to guess?”

Instead of longer prose, you provide decision-making material:

  • Examples of what you like
  • Constraints you must follow
  • Your real situation
  • Grounding references

When the model stops guessing, it stops sounding generic.

3) The Missing Piece: Where Should Context Live?

“Give it more context” raises a practical question:

System prompt or user prompt?

  • System prompt: stable rules and preferences
  • User prompt: task-specific details

Mixing these layers is one of the fastest ways to get inconsistent results.

4) The 4 Types of Context That Actually Improve Results

  1. Past preferences — concrete examples of “good”
  2. Style preferences — do / avoid rules
  3. Specific scenario — goals and constraints
  4. References — trusted grounding material

5) A Mini Story and an Example

A family member complained that AI trip plans always felt the same: touristy, packed, unrealistic.

They rewrote the prompt again and again.

Nothing changed.

Then they pasted two itineraries they loved from past trips plus a short list of non-negotiables.

The next plan finally felt personal.

System Prompt (Permanent Preferences)

You are my practical planning assistant.

  • Default to realistic, doable plans
  • Question feasibility at every step
  • Output: recommendation → options → checklist → risks

My usual preferences:

  • Local food over museums
  • Fewer stops, more depth
  • Comfort over optimization

User Prompt (This Task Only)

Task: Plan a 3-day Tokyo trip for two people.

Scenario

  • Dates: March 14–16
  • Budget: mid-range
  • Hotel: Shinjuku
  • Constraints: slow mornings, shopping, one nice dinner

Style Preferences

  • 3–4 main items per day
  • Food near each area
  • “Too tired?” backup option

If you haven’t tried a context engineering approach, give it a try for a task you repeat regularly.
