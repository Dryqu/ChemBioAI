From Prompt Engineering to Context Engineering: How to Get Better AI Results

December 24, 2025 • By Yi Qu

You can write a beautiful, detailed prompt and still get a disappointing answer. Not because you did it “wrong,” but because the real lever has shifted.

The new advantage isn’t longer prompts. It’s context engineering—and understanding where your context should live.

1) Why “Perfect Prompts” Matter Less Than They Used To

Early AI systems needed extremely detailed instructions to perform well. You had to spell out everything step by step.

But modern models have learned common request patterns from years of interaction. Platforms also improve the surrounding workflow—defaults, guardrails, tool use—so you often get acceptable results with less instruction.

The catch: “acceptable” is not “reliable.” If you want outputs that are consistently useful (and not generic), you need a different approach.

2) The Real Shift: Context Engineering

Traditional prompting is mostly about wording: “How do I say it so the model understands?”

Context engineering is about inputs: “What information does the model need so it doesn’t have to guess?”

Instead of writing a longer prompt describing your preferences, you give the model what humans use to make good decisions:

  • examples of what you like,
  • constraints you must follow,
  • your real situation,
  • and references that ground the answer.

When the model stops guessing, it stops sounding generic.

3) The Missing Piece: Where Should Context Live?

“Give it more context” raises a practical question: system prompt or user prompt?

Most AI systems treat prompts like a hierarchy:

  • System prompt (highest priority): stable rules that should apply every time
  • User prompt (lower priority): today’s task and details

4) The 4 Types of Context That Actually Improve Results

Most “better outputs” come from these four inputs:

  1. Your past preferences (examples)

    What “good” looks like for you—sample itineraries, messages, plans, decisions, etc.

  2. Your style preferences (do/avoid rules)

    Simple vs detailed, budget vs comfort, fast-paced vs relaxed, no red-eyes, no museums, etc.

  3. Your specific scenario (goal + constraints)

    Who’s going, when, budget, mobility, time limits, priorities.

  4. Relevant references (grounding material)

    Lists you trust, schedules you already have, must-visit places, constraints from tickets, maps, or notes.

5) A Mini Story and An Example

My family member used AI to plan trips and complained that every itinerary looked the same: touristy, overstuffed, and unrealistic. They kept rewriting the prompt—more rules, more emphasis, more “please be practical.”

Nothing changed.

Then they did one small thing: they pasted two itineraries they actually loved from past trips, plus a short list of non-negotiables (pace, food priorities, budget range). The next plan finally felt like it was made for them.

Here’s a template that uses all four context types—and puts them in the right layer.

System Prompt (Permanent Preferences)

You are my practical planning assistant.

  • Default to clear, realistic plans (not fantasy itineraries).
  • Ask yourself: “Is this doable in real life?”
  • Output format: (1) a short recommendation, (2) 2–3 options, (3) a simple checklist, (4) what could go wrong + fixes.

My preferences (usually true):

  • I like local food and walking neighborhoods more than museums.
  • I prefer fewer stops, more time per place.
  • I value comfort and simplicity over “maximizing everything.”

User Prompt (This Task Only)

Task: Plan a 3-day trip to Tokyo for two people.

(1) Scenario

  • Dates: March 14–16
  • Budget: mid-range (comfortable but not luxury)
  • Hotel: near Shinjuku (already booked)
  • Constraints: no super early mornings; one afternoon reserved for shopping; one nice dinner

(2) Past Preferences (examples of what “good” looks like)

Here are two mini itineraries I loved—match this pacing:

  • Example A: “Day starts at 10am, one main neighborhood, one anchor meal, one sunset spot, back by 9pm.”
  • Example B: “Pick 2–3 highlights only. Leave time for wandering. Include one ‘backup plan’ if it rains.”

(3) Style Preferences (for this plan)

  • Keep each day to 3–4 main items max
  • Include food suggestions near each area
  • Add a “Too tired?” alternative for each day

(4) References

  • Must-do list: sushi omakase (one meal), a quiet coffee shop, a night view
  • Avoid list: theme parks, packed tour buses
  • Notes: I like places similar to cozy alleyways and small izakayas

If you haven’t tried context engineering approach, I would recommend you give it a try for a task you might repeat regularly.
